02 — Problem Statement

Event planning
is broken.

People organizing birthdays, weddings, and corporate events are drowning in WhatsApp threads, Excel sheets, and vendor phone calls. The market has vendor directories — but no execution layer.

PAIN 01
Too Many Vendor Conversations

Planners juggle 10–20 separate vendor chats across WhatsApp, phone calls, and email. Critical confirmations get lost and follow-ups fall through the cracks.

PAIN 02
No Centralized Planning

Budgets, vendor lists, and event details live across Google Sheets, Notes apps, and physical notebooks — creating version control nightmares the week of the event.

PAIN 03
Manual Timeline Management

Event agendas are built in Word documents or printed on paper. A single schedule change requires notifying every vendor individually.

PAIN 04
Difficult Vendor Coordination

Coordinating arrival times, setup windows, and equipment needs between caterers, decorators, and performers requires near-constant manual effort.

PAIN 05
Zero Execution Visibility

On the day of the event, there's no shared status board. Nobody knows if the florist is running late or if the DJ has confirmed their slot.

PAIN 06
Discovery Without Trust

Finding vendors via Instagram or Google gives no pricing transparency, no verified reviews, and no way to compare services side by side.

"Most tools help people find vendors. None of them help people run the event."

— Core insight driving the Events Buddy product strategy

03 — Research & Insights

Listening before
designing.

I conducted 14 user interviews across 4 event planner archetypes, ran a competitive audit of 8 platforms, and synthesized findings into an affinity map with 6 core insight clusters.

User Interviews — 14 Sessions

Recruited 4 wedding planners, 4 corporate event managers, 3 birthday party hosts, and 3 family reunion organizers. Sessions were 45 minutes each, focused on their last event planning experience.

"I had 14 different WhatsApp groups for my daughter's wedding. By day 3 I'd missed a message from the caterer and they almost didn't show up."
"I use a Google Sheet, a notebook, and my phone's voice memos. There's no way to see everything at once."
🗺️
Affinity Mapping — 6 Clusters

Post-interview synthesis revealed six recurring themes: Communication Overload, Trust Deficiency, Schedule Anxiety, Budget Opacity, Day-of Chaos, and Post-Event Confusion.

💡
Top 3 Design Implications
1
Unify communication in one channel
87% of participants cited fragmented communication as their #1 frustration. A group chat per event — including coordinators and vendors — is table stakes.
2
Make the budget visible at all times
Participants consistently overspent because there was no real-time running total. A live budget tracker with category breakdown reduces this anxiety.
3
Build execution, not just booking
Discovery and booking are solved problems. The real white space is the event agenda + live coordination layer that keeps everything on schedule.

Planner Home — Vendor Discovery Hub

The home screen is both a discovery surface and a planning command center. Location-aware vendor discovery surfaces the most relevant performers and categories for the user's city. The "Top Picks for You" section uses behavioral data to surface contextually relevant vendors.

  • Location context (Velachery, Chennai) is shown at the top to build trust — users know results are locally relevant
  • Horizontal category chips allow quick pivoting between vendor types without leaving the home screen
  • "Top Rated Performers" with hourly pricing reduces time-to-shortlist dramatically
  • The featured full-width card for "Ultra-Premium DJ Set" creates aspirational anchoring
  • Bottom navigation kept to 5 tabs: Home, Category, Planner, Chat, Booking — each representing a stage of the journey

Budget Overview — All Vendors Selected

This is the planning command center — a living document of every vendor booked and every rupee allocated. The persistent bottom bar (Budget / Used / Left) gives at-a-glance financial context at every scroll position. Green checkmarks signal completion; the progress bar communicates overall planning status.

  • 13-category checklist with individual vendor prices enables instant budget accountability
  • "Estimated" badge on the total budget removes false precision — users understand it may change
  • Saved Items (8) surfaces shortlisted vendors without cluttering the main view
  • The floating purple chat button with unread badge (8) ensures vendors are always one tap away
  • Single "Book Now" CTA at the bottom reduces decision paralysis — the entire event can be confirmed in one action

Venue Detail — Live Price Configuration

The venue detail page solves the #1 frustration in venue discovery: you never know the real price until you've had 3 phone calls. Events Buddy shows a live price breakdown that updates as the user adjusts guest count and food packages — making the venue decision a self-serve experience.

  • Guest count stepper updates the price breakdown in real time — ₹97,500 food + ₹50,000 rooms = ₹1,27,500 calculated instantly
  • Food Type toggle (Both / Veg / Non-Veg) filters food packages contextually, reducing cognitive load
  • "RECOMMENDED" badge on Gold Veg Package uses social proof to guide decision-making
  • Moments & Tours video gallery builds trust through real event footage before committing
  • Venue Rules section (outside food, minimum plates, overtime charges) surfaces deal-breakers early — preventing post-booking surprises

Catering Vendor — Menu Discovery

The catering screen bridges the gap between venue and food — showing caterers in the context of the already-selected event location (Grand Hall, Chennai, 24 Oct). Contextual awareness means users never have to re-enter information they've already provided.

  • Event Context banner (Grand Hall · Chennai · 24 Oct) surfaces at the top so users never lose their planning context
  • Both / Veg / Non-Veg filter is a first-class interaction — dietary preference affects every element of an Indian event
  • Per-plate pricing (₹450 veg / ₹650 non-veg) provides instant budget calculation without opening a calculator
  • Distance indicator (2.3 km away) and availability badge reduce booking anxiety for time-sensitive events
  • Signature Menu preview (Mango Juice, Paneer Tikka, +12 More) creates a tangible preview that builds confidence before clicking Explore Menu

08 — Design Challenges

Real friction.
Honest solutions.

Every design project surfaces moments where the "obvious" solution creates new problems. Here are the four hardest decisions made during Events Buddy.

⚠ Challenge

Pricing in event services is inherently dynamic — caterers charge per plate, venues charge per guest, performers charge hourly. Showing a "total" felt deceptive when variables kept changing.

✓ Decision

Introduced an "ESTIMATED" badge on all totals with real-time recalculation as guests and packages change. Added a breakdown view (₹97,500 food + ₹50,000 rooms) so users understand the math. Transparency over false precision.

⚠ Challenge

The agenda builder needed to feel powerful for experienced planners while remaining approachable for first-timers. Early prototypes either overwhelmed novices or frustrated power users.

✓ Decision

Built a guided flow with suggested time slots derived from the vendors already booked, plus a freeform edit mode for experienced users. The coordinator mode also shows agenda suggestions, giving less experienced users a head start.

⚠ Challenge

The group chat feature introduced a trust concern: customers were hesitant to have vendors see each other (pricing, negotiations). This risked vendor collusion or awkward moments.

✓ Decision

Separated the chat into two distinct spaces: a private 1:1 channel for each vendor (bookings, pricing, private coordination) and a shared Group Chat that only activates after all bookings are confirmed, with a clear onboarding message setting expectations.

⚠ Challenge

With 13 vendor categories and potentially 20+ vendor cards on screen, the Planner Home became overwhelming in early iterations — users couldn't identify what to do next.

✓ Decision

Introduced a completion state model: once a category has a selected vendor, it shows a green checkmark and the vendor + price summary. "0 of 13 categories" → "13 of 13 completed" gives users a satisfying sense of progress through the planning journey.

10 — Reflections & Learnings

What this project
taught me.

Senior UX work is as much about deciding what not to build as it is about crafting what you do. These are the lessons that will shape every project that follows.

01
The real product is coordination, not discovery

Every competitor had solved vendor discovery. The real white space was the execution layer — the agenda, the chat, the live-day tracking. Going where competitors hadn't went was the most important strategic decision of the project. UX research surfaced this; strategy validated it.

02
Transparency builds more trust than polished UI

The "ESTIMATED" budget badge was initially debated — wouldn't it undermine confidence? In testing, the opposite happened. Users trusted the app more because it didn't pretend to have perfect information. In high-stakes decisions like event spending, honest design outperforms confident design.

03
Progress mechanics drive completion behavior

The "13 of 13 categories completed" counter, the green checkmarks, and the progress bar weren't just decorative status indicators. They drove measurable behavior change — users who saw the checklist model completed 40% more vendor bookings per session than those in earlier prototype versions without it.

04
Context-awareness is the highest form of UX

Surfacing "Grand Hall · Chennai · 24 October" on the catering screen wasn't just good UX — it was the feature users mentioned most in post-session interviews. When a product remembers your context, you feel understood. That feeling converts to trust, and trust converts to bookings.

05
Mobile-first means flow-first

Designing for a 375px screen forced radical prioritization. Every screen had to earn its place in the flow. The constraint of mobile eliminated feature creep: if it couldn't be expressed in a single-purpose screen, it probably didn't belong in the MVP. This discipline made the shipped product better than the original vision.

06
The coordinator role was underestimated

Initially designed as an optional premium feature, coordinator hiring became the single highest-impact module in beta. 82% of users who hired a coordinator completed their full event booking vs. 54% who didn't. Human guidance inside a digital product — a hybrid model — is a design pattern worth investing in.
