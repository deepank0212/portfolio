01 — Project Overview

One source of truth for who works, when, and where.

RosterOS is an enterprise web application that gives hospitals a single, real-time view of their entire workforce — turning a fragile chain of spreadsheets, WhatsApp messages, and printed rotas into one governed system used by everyone from the Super Admin to the bedside nurse.

What it is

A roster management platform for scheduling, shift assignment, attendance, leave, overtime, and workforce planning — accessed by role-specific dashboards.

Why it matters

Staffing decisions in a hospital are patient-safety decisions. The right nurse-to-patient ratio on every shift is the difference between safe and unsafe care.

The goal

Cut administrative effort, eliminate scheduling conflicts, and give every role the visibility they need — on a UI that stays usable under real operational pressure.

Domain primer

What is a Roster Management System?

A roster (or "rota") is the published plan of which staff member works which shift. A Roster Management System operationalizes that plan end-to-end: it builds schedules from demand and skill rules, assigns people to shifts, tracks who actually showed up, processes leave and overtime, and reports on coverage and cost.

Why hospitals specifically need it

Care is delivered 24/7/365 across wards with different acuity, skill-mix, and legal minimum staffing. A missed shift isn't a missed meeting — it's an unsafe ward. Manual tools cannot keep up with the volume, the constraints, or the speed of change.

Operational risks without it

  • Understaffed shifts that breach safe nurse-to-patient ratios
  • Double-booked or fatigued staff working unsafe consecutive hours
  • Uncontrolled overtime and agency spend
  • Leave approved into already-thin coverage
  • No audit trail for compliance or incident review

KPIs that define success

02 — Problem Statement

Hospitals were running their workforce on spreadsheets, paper, and WhatsApp.

Staff schedules, shift assignments, and leave requests lived across spreadsheets, paper records, messaging groups, and manual communication — creating scheduling conflicts, delayed leave approvals, and a constant administrative drag.

Hospital administrators struggle to quickly identify who is on duty, who is on leave, and whether enough staff are available for each shift. Staff lack any centralized place to view schedules, manage leave, and receive real-time updates.

As hospitals grow, managing multiple departments, staff roles, and shift rotations becomes increasingly complex — leading to operational inefficiencies and potential staffing shortages across the organization.

For Administrators

  • Manual shift scheduling
  • Difficulty tracking staff availability
  • Leave approval bottlenecks
  • Lack of centralized visibility
  • Scheduling conflicts

For Staff

  • Unclear shift schedules
  • Delayed leave approvals
  • Communication gaps
  • No centralized leave history

For Organizations

  • Limited workforce visibility
  • Inefficient resource planning
  • Increased operational overhead

How might we help hospital administrators manage staff shifts and leave requests efficiently — while giving staff a simple way to view schedules, request leave, and stay informed about workplace updates?

03 — Research Phase

We grounded the design in how staffing actually works on the floor.

Because patient safety is at stake, assumptions were treated as hypotheses to validate — not facts. Research combined stakeholder interviews, competitive teardown, and shadowing of the real scheduling ritual.

Research approach & assumptions

  • Stakeholder interviews with HR, supervisors, and department managers
  • Contextual shadowing of a full roster-build cycle
  • Competitive analysis of workforce-management tools
  • Assumption: supervisors want control, not full automation — validated
  • Assumption: nurses mostly need read access + self-service requests — validated

Key findings that shaped every decision

Finding 01

The grid is the product

Supervisors think in a week-by-staff matrix. The scheduling grid had to feel faster and safer than their spreadsheet, or they wouldn't switch.

Finding 02

Conflicts must be visible live

Errors caught while assigning — not at month-end — were the single biggest source of trust. Validation needed to be inline and immediate.

Finding 03

Every role sees a different hospital

The same data needs five different framings. One dashboard could not serve a Staff Nurse and a Super Admin equally — role-aware views were essential.

06 — Design Process

From paper sketches to a validated grid.

The scheduling grid went through the most iteration — it's the heart of the product, and the place where complexity had to disappear into something that feels effortless.

Wireframing

Low-fidelity layouts pressure-tested the navigation model and the grid's row/column logic before any visual design. Goal: prove the structure, not the pixels.

Layout exploration

We tested calendar-style, list-style, and matrix-style scheduling. The staff-by-day matrix won — it matched supervisors' existing mental model and showed coverage at a glance.

Design iterations

Iteration focused on density, inline validation, and how to show conflicts without alarm fatigue. Status color, cell states, and the warning system were refined across several rounds of feedback.

08 — Challenges

Hard problems we had to design around.

Complex scheduling workflows

Balancing demand, skill-mix, hours, and fairness in one grid without overwhelming the user. Solved with templates, sensible defaults, and progressive disclosure of rules.

Workforce planning at scale

Hundreds of staff across many wards. The UI had to stay scannable and fast — driving the dense-table system and column-level coverage totals.

Attendance & reality drift

The plan and what actually happens never fully match. We designed variance as a first-class, visible state rather than an error to hide.

Designing for five roles

One system, five very different jobs-to-be-done. Role-aware dashboards and a clear permission matrix kept each experience focused without forking the product.
