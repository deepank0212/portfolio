Project Context

Built for the people keeping hospitals alive

Healthcare workforce management is one of the most complexity-dense domains in enterprise software. Every shift change carries patient safety implications. This system was designed to absorb that complexity — so nurses, supervisors, and HR managers don't have to.

My responsibilities

As the sole UI/UX designer, I owned the full design process from discovery through developer handoff — user interviews, IA, wireframes, high-fidelity screens, component library, and annotated Figma handoff files.

Project scope

Four distinct user types — staff nurses, supervisors, HR managers, and system administrators — each with different access levels, workflows, and mental models. Designing role-based access with a coherent visual language was the central challenge.

Problem Statement

The system that wasn't a system

Healthcare scheduling in most mid-sized hospitals still runs on Excel spreadsheets, WhatsApp group chats, and paper shift boards. The inefficiency is staggering — and the human cost is real.

"I spend three hours every Sunday rebuilding next week's roster. Every time someone calls in sick, I have to manually check who's available, text multiple nurses, and hope someone replies before the shift starts."

— Senior Ward Supervisor, discovery interview
📋
Manual roster building

Supervisors spend 3–5 hours weekly constructing schedules with no automated conflict detection or coverage validation.

⚔️
Shift conflicts & double-booking

Without a centralised system, nurses are routinely scheduled for overlapping shifts, leading to burnout and understaffed wards.

🗂️
Fragmented leave tracking

Leave requests via WhatsApp. No audit trail, no balance visibility, approvals handled through text messages.

👁️
No workforce visibility

HR managers had zero real-time visibility into department coverage, making budget planning and compliance reporting nearly impossible.

🔔
Zero proactive notification

Staff learned about schedule changes through informal channels. Shift updates were regularly missed.

🔄
No shift swap mechanism

Every swap required full supervisor mediation, creating a bottleneck that discouraged flexible scheduling.

Design challenge

How do we design a role-aware workforce management platform that reduces administrative overhead by 60%, gives all stakeholders real-time visibility, and is intuitive enough for clinical staff who have never used scheduling software before?

Business Goals

What success looks like

Operational
Reduce scheduling time

Cut roster prep from 3–5 hours to under 30 minutes through automated conflict detection and intelligent recommendations.

Compliance
Audit-ready records

Create an immutable, timestamped record of all scheduling decisions, leave approvals, and attendance data.

Staff Experience
Self-service empowerment

Give nursing staff visibility into schedules, leave balances, and the ability to request swaps without full supervisor mediation.

User Research

Listening before designing

Two weeks embedded in research — interviewing clinical staff, shadowing shift handovers, and auditing existing scheduling artifacts — before opening Figma.

Research methods
🎤
Semi-structured interviews

12 participants across 4 roles, 45–60 min each. Goal: surface mental models and workflow breakpoints.

👁️
Contextual observation

Shadowed 3 supervisors through Monday morning roster-building. Documented every tool opened.

📊
Survey validation

48 responses from nursing staff across two departments to quantify pain points.

Key finding 01

78% of nurses checked their schedule via WhatsApp screenshots rather than any official source.

Key finding 02

Supervisors lost 4.2 hours per week on scheduling-related communication — not scheduling itself.

Key finding 03

100% of HR managers couldn't generate a staffing report without 2 days of manual data collection.

Core user pain points
😩
Cognitive overload

Supervisors tracking availability, skill mix, holiday entitlements, and departmental policies simultaneously — all in their heads.

🌀
Invisible workload

Neither nurses nor HR could see how work was distributed. Burnout was chronic but impossible to flag through data.

⏱️
Reactive, not proactive

Every scheduling decision was a reaction to a crisis — sick calls, no-shows — rather than a planned, balanced allocation.

Wireframes

Structure before style

Built in low fidelity first, tested with 5 participants using paper prototypes, then refined before moving to high fidelity. Two key decisions validated at this stage:

Decision: Bottom tab navigation

Tested hamburger menu vs. bottom tabs. Bottom tabs won — clinical staff use phones with gloves on and need large, predictable touch targets. Home · Calendar · Leave · Alerts · Profile maps to the five most common tasks.

Decision: Today-first home screen

Tested agenda-first vs. status-first vs. today-first layouts. Staff consistently wanted to know "what is my shift right now?" first. Today's shift became the dominant hero element.

Final Solution

Every screen, explained

Purpose, UX decisions, and business rationale behind each key screen in the final design system.

Screen 01 — Home Dashboard
Staff View
1
Duty status hero card

Gradient card dominates to instantly communicate duty status. Color shifts to muted palette when user is off duty — answering the most frequent question nurses had without any interaction.

2
Inline clock-out CTA

Clock-out lives inside the shift card, not a separate module. Reduces friction for the most time-sensitive action, preventing missed clock-outs that cause payroll errors.

3
Tri-stat summary row

Leave balance, upcoming shifts, and pending requests are visible on load — the three metrics nurses checked most frequently per research. Zero navigation required.

4
This-week strip + leave tracker

A horizontal 4-day preview keeps the upcoming week glanceable, while the pending leave request shows a live three-stage progress bar so staff stop chasing supervisors for status updates.

5
Recent activity feed

Swap approvals, timesheet submissions, training deadlines, and on-call assignments surface chronologically with relative timestamps — a single place to catch up after time off.

Business value · Reduces "what am I doing today?" queries to supervisors by an estimated 40%. Clock-out compliance improves payroll accuracy directly.
Screen 02 — Shift Calendar
Staff + Supervisor
1
Colour-encoded calendar dots

A short underline bar per date cell uses the shift-type color. Scans faster than text labels and works at small sizes. The active date uses a filled circle to prevent confusion with today's date.

2
Tap-to-reveal shift detail

Tapping any date reveals a contextual detail panel below the calendar — no modal, no navigation. Keeps the user anchored in the calendar while seeing shift details.

3
Month-level shift summary

Below the calendar: total shifts by type (8 Morning / 8 Evening / 4 Night / 10 Off). Instant workload overview without a separate report screen.

4
Persistent legend

A small color-key sits permanently beneath the grid, so the semantic system never requires onboarding or a tooltip to interpret.

5
Dual-action detail card

The selected-day card pairs "Request swap" and "View details" side by side, putting the two most common follow-up actions one tap away from any date.

Business value · Self-service schedule visibility eliminates the most common supervisor interruption — "can you send me my schedule?" — consuming an estimated 45 minutes per supervisor per week.

Challenges & Solutions

Where the hard problems lived

Challenge 01
Role-based complexity vs. simplicity

Four user roles needed access to the same underlying data but with radically different actions, views, and permissions. How do you build one navigation system that doesn't overwhelm any single user type?

Solution
Progressive disclosure by role

Bottom navigation is identical across all roles, but each tab renders a role-appropriate view. A nurse's Calendar shows only their schedule. A supervisor's Calendar is a full department grid. The mental model is preserved; content depth scales with permission level.

Challenge 02
Making shift types instantly scannable

The calendar needed to convey five shift types at very small sizes in a single glance. Text labels inside cells were too small; icons were too ambiguous.

Solution
Semantic color system with underline bars

A persistent 3px underline bar per date cell uses the shift-type color, consistent across every screen in the product. Users build unconscious pattern recognition. A persistent legend ensures onboarding without a manual.

Challenge 03
Adoption by non-digital-native users

~30% of nursing staff were over 50 and had never used a mobile scheduling app. Any design requiring tutorials would be abandoned immediately under time pressure.

Solution
Zero-instruction design principle

Every key action visible on the primary screen — no hidden menus, no long-press gestures. Button labels use plain clinical language. Usability tests with older staff showed task completion without any instruction within 90 seconds of first use.

Conclusion

Healthcare workforce management, redesigned from the ground up

The Roster Management System demonstrates what happens when enterprise software is designed with the same intentionality applied to consumer apps. The clinical environment demands clarity, speed, and reliability — not just functionality.

Every decision — from the shift colour system to the bottom navigation architecture — was made in service of the people who can't afford to waste time figuring out software.
